Module 01

The Origins of Profanity

What is it, where does it come from, and who decides?

💬 Before You Read

Think of a word you wouldn't say at a job interview. Now think of a word you wouldn't say at a funeral. Are they the same word? Probably not. Why? That gap — the difference between inappropriate at work and genuinely taboo — is where this module lives.

Section 1

What Are We Even Talking About?

Before we can explain where profanity comes from, we have to figure out what it actually is. This turns out to be harder than it sounds, which is either reassuring or mildly depressing depending on your relationship with the subject.

Profanity is a surprisingly slippery term. Broadly, it refers to language that violates social norms of decency — words that are offensive not just because of what they literally mean but because of what they culturally represent. Eldridge's definition via Britannica frames it as "language that is considered socially offensive due to being vulgar, obscene, or irreverent" (Eldridge, n.d.). That works, but note what it smuggles in: socially offensive. Offensive to whom? Determined by whom?

Here is a cluster of related terms you need to be able to distinguish. They get used interchangeably in casual conversation but mean different things. Click each one to see the definition and an example.

Profanity ▾

Language that violates norms of reverence, especially toward the sacred. Historically religious in origin; now broader in scope.

"Jesus Christ!" when you spill coffee — invoking the sacred in an everyday, irreverent context.
Obscenity ▾

Language that is sexually explicit or otherwise morally offensive. Legal systems tend to define this one — with significant disagreement about where the line falls.

The seven words George Carlin famously argued you couldn't say on American television.
Blasphemy ▾

Specifically religious profanity. Invoking or disrespecting the divine. Was historically the most severe form of verbal transgression in many cultures.

In medieval England, swearing on God's wounds ("Zounds!") was considered deeply blasphemous.
Slurs ▾

Identity-based insults. Designed to dehumanize or marginalize based on who someone is — race, gender, sexuality, ability, class.

A racial slur targets not one person's behaviour but their membership in a group. This is categorically different from other profanity.
Taboo Language ▾

The broadest category. Any language that is culturally forbidden or deeply stigmatized in a given community. All profanity is taboo, but not all taboo language is profanity.

In some cultures, speaking the name of the dead is taboo — not because it's "rude" but because it carries spiritual risk.
Expletives ▾

Words or phrases used as filler or for emphasis, often without literal meaning. The brain reaches for them automatically under stress or surprise.

"Oh my god" when you see a surprise. "Christ" when you stub your toe. The sacred invoked not to transgress but to release.
Minced Oaths ▾

Softened versions of profanity. Technically acceptable but structurally doing the same job as their stronger counterparts.

"Shoot," "heck," "fudge," "dang," "gosh" — everyone knows what these are standing in for. The social work is the same; the cost is reduced.

You could think of these categories as different intensities of the same thing, or as different flavours. A slur is doing something fundamentally different from an expletive. Both are offensive, but the mechanism and the harm are different. In the interest of clarity, we will sometimes use these terms interchangeably but at moments will also need to be precise about which type of swearing we're analyzing.

Section 2

Where Does 'Profanity' Even Come From?

The etymology — the origin of a word — matters here. Profane comes from the Latin profanus: literally "outside the temple" (pro = before/outside, fanum = temple or sacred space). In ancient Rome, the profanus was anything common, everyday, or not set apart as sacred. To be profane was simply to be outside the sacred precinct.

The sociologist Émile Durkheim gave this spatial metaphor a structural foundation. In his analysis of religion and social life, Durkheim argued that all societies divide the world into two domains: the sacred — things set apart, invested with collective reverence, surrounded by prohibitions — and the profane — the ordinary, the everyday, the instrumental. For Durkheim, this distinction is not theological but social: whatever a community treats as sacred becomes sacred through collective belief and ritual (Durkheim, 1912/1995). The power of a sacred word or object comes from the community that recognizes it as such.

This matters for understanding swearing. When we use a word that transgresses the sacred — invoking divine names carelessly, dragging holy language into vulgar contexts — we are violating a socially maintained boundary. The offense is real not because the cosmos has been disturbed, but because the community's shared framework has been disrespected. The power of a swear word is always borrowed from the community that makes it powerful.

This etymology tells us something important: profanity has always been about transgression. It is not just offensive speech. It is speech that crosses a boundary — a boundary that someone has declared should not be crossed.

The word taboo adds another layer. Borrowed from Polynesian languages (specifically Tongan and Fijian), tapu means something that is spiritually restricted or forbidden. The word entered English in the 18th century through accounts of Pacific exploration. But borrowing this concept carries its own complications worth pausing on.

📌 A Note on Colonization and Borrowed Concepts

The word "taboo" entered the English language largely through the writings of Captain James Cook, who encountered tapu during his Pacific voyages in the 1770s. The appropriation of Indigenous concepts by colonial-era Europeans is a complicated history. Cook and his crew were part of an enterprise of territorial and cultural domination — the same system that would later forcibly suppress the very languages and practices these terms described.

That said, the concept of tapu can still be useful for understanding profanity, provided we use it carefully and resist the urge to flatten it. In many Polynesian and other Indigenous traditions, tapu does not merely mean "socially inappropriate." It describes a genuine spiritual force: words, names, and places that carry power, that can bless, bind, harm, or heal. The Western tendency to reduce all of this to "etiquette" or "rudeness" is a kind of cultural flattening that loses the concept's depth. We can use the idea while acknowledging its origins and the violence through which it was extracted from its context.

This is worth thinking more carefully about. In many Indigenous and Pacific cultures, tapu is not simply a social rule. It describes a genuine spiritual force. Words, names, and places could carry tapu — they were not just symbolically off-limits but were understood as powerful, potentially dangerous. The Western reduction of all of this to "rudeness" loses something essential about what a forbidden word actually is.

🌍 Global Lens

The Japanese concept of kotodama (言霊) — literally "word spirit" — holds that words carry real spiritual power. They are not arbitrary labels; they are forces that can bless, bind, harm, or heal. This is not superstition but a structurally different theory of how language works.

The Yoruba concept of egan describes language that is deliberately transgressive — used to destabilize social hierarchies. And in many Indigenous North American traditions, certain names and words are not spoken aloud not because they are "bad" but because they are powerful and their power must be treated with respect.

Taken together, these frameworks suggest that the "power" of profanity is not a Western invention — it is a near-universal human phenomenon, even if its specific form varies dramatically across cultures and traditions.

Section 3

How We Study Profanity: Three Analytical Frames

This course approaches profanity through three overlapping analytical frames. Think of them as three cameras pointing at the same thing from different angles: the physiological, the historical, and the linguistic. These frameworks draw on a range of scholars, but Melissa Mohr's history of swearing, Holy Sh*t (2013), offers a useful orienting claim: profanity across history has revolved around two poles — the holy and the shit. The sacred and the bodily. What a culture treats as spiritually elevated and what it treats as disgustingly corporeal — these are the two territories from which the power of swear words has always been drawn.

Our three frames give us the tools to examine how that power works.

Frame 1: The Body

Physiological Analysis

Profanity affects us physically. Taboo words are processed differently in the brain than neutral words. They tend to be remembered better, recalled more easily under stress, and — as we will get into in Module 2 — have measurable effects on pain tolerance and emotional regulation. This is not metaphorical.

The limbic system — the part of the brain responsible for emotion and survival responses — processes taboo language in ways it does not for neutral words. This is why people with certain neurological conditions like Tourette's syndrome may experience coprolalia (involuntary swearing): the limbic system bypasses the brain's usual social filters.

The Stroop Effect offers a related window. In a standard Stroop test, you name the colour of ink a word is printed in rather than reading the word itself. It's harder than it sounds because your brain automatically processes meaning. Versions using taboo words show even greater interference — the emotional charge of the word makes it harder to look past. The word's power is not just social; it is neurological.

Frame 2: History & Society

Historical & Social Analysis

Profanity changes over time, and what changes is what a culture holds sacred. Mohr (2013) traces this arc across Western history, showing that the vocabulary of swearing follows shifts in what a society treats as inviolable. Click through the timeline in Section 3 below to see this pattern in action.

The core insight: swear words target what we hold most sacred. As societies change what they hold sacred, profanity changes accordingly. When religious authority was absolute, blasphemy was the worst thing you could say. As religious authority declined, bodily and sexual terms took over. As identity and dignity have become central to social discourse, slurs now carry the weight that "goddamn" once did.

The social frame also asks: who decides what counts as offensive? Laws, institutions, communities, and families all have a stake in that question — and none of them are neutral.

Frame 3: Language Itself

Linguistic Analysis

At the purely linguistic level, profanity is interesting for reasons that have nothing to do with offense. Sound patterns, the mechanics of how words acquire meaning, and the distinction between using and mentioning a word are all in play.

Linguists have noted that swear words in English cluster around specific phoneme types — plosives (hard sounds: k, p, t, d, b) and fricatives (friction sounds: f, sh, s) — and that softer sounds (approximants: l, r, w) are notably rare. The sounds themselves do some of the work.

Words also carry two kinds of meaning: denotation (the literal dictionary definition) and connotation (the emotional, historical, social baggage). Profanity almost always works through connotation — the power is not in the literal meaning but in what the word historically signals.

Dominant form: Divine curses & oaths

Ancient Greece and Rome

Swearing invoked gods as witnesses, guarantors, and weapons. Sacred names were used to call down divine harm on enemies or bind agreements in cosmic force. The divine was the most powerful force available, so divine names were the most powerful verbal weapons. To swear was to invoke cosmic authority against someone — not mere rudeness but a genuine act of power.

Mohr traces how blasphemous language in this period was not just "rude" — it was a form of magic. The word was the act.

Dominant form: Blasphemy & sacrilege

The Middle Ages

Christianity was the organizing principle of European society. The Church was the law, the school, the court, and the cultural centre simultaneously. Violations of the sacred — swearing on God's wounds (Zounds! = God's wounds; Bloody = By Our Lady), misusing holy names, profaning the Eucharist — were the worst linguistic crimes imaginable. The power of the Church was the power of language itself.

Mohr argues that medieval swearing was extraordinarily creative and imaginative — the vocabulary of the sacred provided a rich canvas for transgression, and transgression was correspondingly severe.

Dominant form: Transition

Renaissance and Enlightenment (~1400–1700)

The gradual decentering of religious authority. The Reformation fractured Christian Europe; the Scientific Revolution reoriented intellectual life around nature rather than scripture. Sexual and bodily terms begin their rise as society's focus shifts from the divine to the human body. The sacred starts to lose its monopoly on transgression — and the body begins to acquire the charge that blasphemy once had.

Shakespeare's plays contain very few truly obscene words by modern standards. The strongest insults of the period were still frequently religious — but the transition is visible in the vocabulary.

Dominant form: Bodily taboos & euphemism explosion

The Victorian Era (~1800s)

Increased anxiety around bodies, bodily functions, and sexuality. Victorian culture developed an elaborate system of euphemism — indirect, polite language to avoid direct reference to things that had become deeply shameful. "Bathroom talk" and body parts became the prime territory of the forbidden. The body was now more shameful than the divine. Piano "limbs," "unmentionables" for trousers, "in an interesting condition" for pregnant — the euphemism treadmill ran at full speed.

This is the era that established many of our current intuitions about what "dirty words" are.

Dominant form: Sexual liberation & the rise of slurs

The 20th Century

Sexual liberation shifts the cultural weight of sexual terms — "fuck" becomes rebellious, not just dirty. George Carlin's "seven dirty words" were primarily sexual and scatological. And crucially: the rise of identity-based slurs as the dominant form of harmful language.

Race, gender, sexuality become the new sacred, and violations of dignity around those identities become the new worst-thing-you-can-say. The n-word during the Civil Rights movement carried something of the same weight that blasphemy carried in the medieval period: a violation of a collectively held sacred — human dignity — with real political stakes.

Dominant form: Identity, dignity & algorithmic censorship

The 21st Century

"Offensive" language is now less about bodies and religion and more about who you're targeting. Slurs based on identity carry the weight that "goddamn" once did. The sacred today is human dignity — and its violation is the most transgressive linguistic act available.

A new dimension: algorithmic content moderation. Platforms now regulate language at unprecedented scale, producing new forms of coded speech and self-censorship. TikTok's "unalive" for suicide, coded language for LGBTQ+ identities — the forbidden word list is maintained not by the Church or the state but by content moderation systems whose criteria are opaque and whose effects fall unevenly across communities.

Mohr's insight is that swear words target what we hold sacred — and as societies change what they hold sacred, the vocabulary of profanity changes accordingly. What does the 21st century pattern tell us about what we now collectively treat as inviolable?

📌 Metalinguistics: Use vs. Mention

When you use a word, you deploy it for its meaning or effect: "You're an asshole." When you mention a word, you treat it as an object of analysis: "The word asshole derives from Old English." This is the use-mention distinction — a concept from metalinguistics, the study of language talking about language itself. It matters enormously in discussions of slurs and profanity. Saying something harmful and studying something harmful are not the same act. Throughout this course, we will frequently be mentioning words — analyzing them as objects — rather than using them. That distinction is not a loophole. It is an intellectual and ethical responsibility.

Section 4

Who Decides?

This is the question this course will keep returning to. Laws, social norms, institutions, communities, families — all of these have a hand in deciding what counts as profanity and what the consequences of using it are. None of these are neutral.

The legal definition is instructive: the FCC in the United States defined profanity in broadcast contexts as "language so grossly offensive to members of the public who actually hear it as to amount to a nuisance." Offensive to the public. But which public? This framing puts the power of definition in the hands of whoever is listening — and historically, "the public" whose sensibilities counted has not been everyone.

🤔 Think About It

Billie Holiday was banned from performing "Strange Fruit" — a song about lynching — on American radio in 1939. The stated reason was that the content was "obscene." NWA's "Fuck tha Police" was the subject of FBI warning letters. George Carlin's "seven dirty words" got a radio station fined by the FCC.

In each case, who decided what counted as "too offensive"? And what were the actual reasons? The word "obscene" does a lot of work in covering up what is really a political choice.

Section 6

The Euphemism Treadmill

A word's power as profanity depends on its tabooness — the social cost attached to using it. What happens when that cost decreases? This is the mechanism behind the euphemism treadmill, a term coined by Steven Pinker (2007) to describe a cycle that plays out repeatedly in the history of language.

The cycle works like this: a concept becomes socially stigmatized. A new, neutral word is introduced to describe it. The new word, through its constant association with the stigmatized concept, gradually absorbs the stigma itself. A new neutral replacement is needed. And so on. The treadmill keeps moving. Classic examples: "idiot," "moron," and "imbecile" were once clinical psychological terms with specific diagnostic meanings. All three became general insults. "Retarded" replaced them as a clinical term, and went the same way. "Intellectual disability" is the current preferred term — and already the earlier terms are sufficiently charged that the replacement is felt as necessary.

The pattern holds across many identity categories. The stigma travels with the concept, not the word. This is why simply renaming something doesn't automatically change how people think about it.

The other side of this coin is semantic amelioration — when a word becomes less offensive over time. "Damn" and "hell" were genuinely shocking in polite Victorian company. Now they appear in PG-rated films. "Bitch," once almost exclusively a gendered slur, appears regularly in music, casual conversation, and in the vocabularies of people who have reclaimed it. Words can lose power as well as gain it — and both processes reveal something about what the surrounding society values.

Module 02

The Purpose of Profanity

Why do people swear, and what is it actually doing?

💬 Before You Read

The next time you stub your toe, drop something heavy on your foot, or get cut off in traffic, notice what comes out. Not because you're about to be judged, but because what you say in that moment reveals something about how swearing actually works. It's not random. It's functional.

Section 1

Profanity Has a Job

One of the most common misreadings of swearing is that it's meaningless: a kind of verbal static produced by people who lack vocabulary or impulse control. This is false, and the evidence against it is plentiful.

Swearing is purposeful behaviour even when unintentional. Timothy Jay (2009), one of the most cited researchers on the psychology of profanity, argues that taboo words serve genuine purposes on two distinct levels — the intrapersonal (helping regulate the speaker's own emotional and physical states) and the interpersonal (managing relationships, power dynamics, and group belonging). The words are not the problem; they are the solution to several different problems simultaneously.

Intrapersonal functions operate within the speaker — swearing as self-regulation.

Emotional release: Swearing provides a pressure valve for negative emotional states. The spontaneous expletive when something goes wrong is not performance — it's regulation. It allows the body to signal distress and begin to process it.
Pain management: Swearing measurably reduces the perception of physical pain (the hypoalgesic effect, covered in Section 3). This is an intrapersonal physiological function — the word is working on your nervous system, not on the people around you.
Stress modulation: Under conditions of high cognitive or emotional load, profanity may help the nervous system regulate its arousal level. The word activates the fight-or-flight system in a controlled way, providing a momentary discharge.
Self-expression: Profanity provides emphasis and specificity that neutral language sometimes cannot. "I'm really frustrated" and "I'm fucking furious" describe different intensities of the same state — the second is more precise, not less sophisticated.

Interpersonal functions operate between speakers — swearing as social action.

Group bonding: Shared profanity signals mutual trust and informality. "We can swear around each other" means "normal social rules are suspended here — we trust each other enough." This is a genuine social function, not incidental.
Emphasis and attention: Profanity commands attention. The introduction of a swear word into otherwise neutral speech shifts the listener's focus and signals that what follows — or just preceded — matters to the speaker. It's a prosodic marker with social weight.
Power and aggression: Abusive swearing positions the speaker as dominant and the target as subordinate. It can intimidate, demean, or dehumanize. This is the interpersonal function that does the most harm — when profanity becomes a weapon directed at a person rather than a situation.
Humour and play: Among friends, profanity often signals that the interaction is informal, playful, and not to be taken at face value. The banter, the roast, the teasing insult — these rely on shared understanding that the words are not to be taken literally. Context makes this legible.
Section 2

Pinker's Five Types of Swearing

We can further subdivide the purposes of profanity. Steven Pinker (2007) identifies five distinct categories of swearing. Understanding these changes how you hear profanity — and how you think about arguments for regulating it. Click each type to expand.

Type 01
Cathartic ▾

Swearing as emotional release. The involuntary "shit!" when you realize you've sent the wrong email. It happens before conscious thought and reliably makes you feel better. The cathartic function is deeply tied to the limbic system — taboo words have a faster path to emotional centres than neutral words. This is why "shoot!" doesn't produce the same effect as the actual word. The neurological response depends on the word carrying actual social risk.

"Fuck!" upon stubbing a toe, spilling coffee, or missing a deadline.
Type 02
Dysphemistic ▾

The opposite of euphemism. Where euphemism softens ("passed away" instead of "died"), dysphemism makes something sound worse, uglier, or rawer than neutral language would. It's language with a point of view — it signals contempt, disgust, or deliberate rejection of a softened framing. When used about people rather than things, it shades quickly into derogation and slurs.

Calling a war a "bloodbath," a meal "slop," or a politician a "hack." Stronger: "white trash," "pig," "slut."
Type 03
Abusive ▾

Directed at people. Designed to harm, intimidate, insult, or dehumanize. This is swearing as weapon — and it is different in character from cathartic or emphatic swearing even when the same words appear. Context makes all the difference: the same word exchanged between close friends joking around and said by a supervisor to an employee are categorically different acts, even if the phonemes are identical.

"Get the fuck out of here." "You're a worthless piece of shit." The intent is damage, not release.
Type 04
Emphatic ▾

Used to intensify, signal surprise, or add force. Here the taboo word functions as an intensifier — it does the same job as "very" or "absolutely" but with more firepower. The profanity is doing grammatical work, not carrying literal meaning. It has migrated from the domain of transgression into the domain of grammar.

"That was fucking brilliant." "I absolutely cannot believe this." The word amplifies; it doesn't offend.
Type 05
Idiomatic ▾

The most everyday category. Figurative, informal, and often not consciously taboo at all in context. These expressions are so embedded in everyday speech that the original transgression has faded. Pinker calls this language's way of acquiring colour and texture over time — the fossilized remains of older transgressions.

"She scared the shit out of me." "What the hell is going on?" "Son of a bitch actually worked." The literal meaning has largely evaporated.
Section 3

The Hypoalgesic Effect: Swearing and Pain

In 2009, Richard Stephens, John Atkins, and Andrew Kingston published a paper that became one of the most cited and most gleefully shared findings in linguistics. Their experiment was simple: participants held their hand in ice-cold water while either repeating a swear word or a neutral word. The swearers lasted significantly longer and reported less pain. This is the hypoalgesic effect: the measurable pain-reduction produced by swearing (Stephens, Atkins, & Kingston, 2009).

The Brain and Swearing — Click a region to learn more

🔴 Limbic System
The emotional core of the brain — responsible for emotional processing, memory, and the fight-or-flight response. Swear words appear to be processed partly here, bypassing the slower, more deliberate pathways used for neutral language. This is why profanity comes out under stress before you've consciously chosen it.
🟠 Amygdala
Part of the limbic system, the amygdala processes emotional responses — particularly fear and arousal. Taboo words trigger heightened amygdala activation compared to neutral words, which explains both their emotional punch and the conditioned anxiety many people feel when hearing them.
🔵 Prefrontal Cortex
Responsible for executive function, impulse control, and social judgement. This is what keeps you from swearing at a job interview — the prefrontal cortex applies the brakes. When it's fatigued, stressed, or bypassed (as in Tourette's), the limbic system's more primitive response gets through.
🟢 Broca's Area
The primary language-production area. Neutral speech is primarily generated here. Interestingly, automatic swearing (the involuntary expletive under pain or shock) may bypass Broca's Area and be generated more directly through subcortical pathways — which is why some stroke patients who lose the ability to form speech can still swear.
🟡 Basal Ganglia
Involved in procedural learning and habitual actions. May play a role in the involuntary nature of coprolalia (involuntary swearing in Tourette's syndrome), functioning as an automatic motor output pathway that bypasses conscious language control. Also connected to the limbic system's emotional circuitry.

The proposed mechanism behind the hypoalgesic effect: swearing activates the body's sympathetic nervous system — the fight-or-flight response — triggering an adrenaline release, increased blood flow to the skin, and other physiological changes that genuinely dull pain perception. The emotional charge of the taboo word is doing real physiological work.

There is an important wrinkle. A 2011 follow-up study by Stephens found that the effect was weaker for participants who swore frequently in daily life. The more you use a word, the less taboo charge it carries — and it is precisely the taboo charge that appears to drive the pain-relief effect. This connects back to Module 1's discussion of how a word's power depends on its forbidden status.

So, counterintuitively: if you want swearing to relieve your pain most effectively, swear less. You're welcome.

🤔 Think About It

The hypoalgesic effect suggests swearing has a measurable benefit — it reduces pain. Yet many institutions work hard to suppress swearing, including in contexts like hospitals, schools, and workplaces where people experience real stress and discomfort. Who benefits from that suppression? What does it do to the people who comply with it?

Section 4

Aversive Classical Conditioning: How We Learn to Swear

How do we develop the emotional response to taboo words in the first place? The mechanism is aversive classical conditioning. From early childhood, certain words are paired with fear, shame, punishment, or disapproval — and through repeated pairing, the word itself begins to trigger that emotional response, regardless of context. You don't have to consciously remember why "f***" is a bad word; your nervous system remembers for you.

This is why profanity retains its emotional punch even for people who intellectually believe there is nothing inherently wrong with any particular word. The conditioning happened before the intellectual framework. It is also why the effect diminishes with repeated exposure — the conditioning can be partially unwound through habituation. And it is why censorship has limited effectiveness: you can bleep the word on television, but the audience knows exactly what word was bleeped. The emotional response is largely the same.

Section 5

Social Functions: Obscenity Factories

Beyond individual psychology, swearing plays important social roles. Gary Alan Fine and Ugo Corte (2024), in their study of profanity in workplace cultures, introduce the concept of the obscenity factory: a workplace or group culture in which profane language is not merely tolerated but is structurally central to how the group functions — how it bonds, maintains hierarchy, and draws boundaries.

Fine and Corte studied trauma doctors in war zones, restaurant cooks, wildland firefighters, and correction officers — environments characterized by high stress, physical danger, intense group interdependence, and significant departures from mainstream professional norms. In each case, they found that profanity served three distinct social functions:

🤝
Affiliation ▾

Shared profanity deepens group membership. Swearing together is a signal of mutual trust and belonging — it says "we are close enough that the normal rules don't apply here." In high-stakes environments, this bond is not trivial. It is part of how group cohesion is maintained under pressure.

📊
Division ▾

Profanity defines the status hierarchy within a group. How, when, and what kind of swearing is permitted signals rank and authority. A senior surgeon who swears freely in the operating room while interns watch their language is enacting hierarchy through profanity. The rules of who can swear at whom are social maps of power.

🚧
Distinction ▾

Group profanity marks insiders from outsiders. A shared vocabulary of profanity — including in-group specific insults, nicknames, and codes — delineates community boundaries. The "front stage" language used with outsiders and the "back stage" language used within the group signal who belongs and who doesn't.

This explains the paradox of swearing being both offensive and bonding. A slur used by an outsider to attack a group is violence. The same word used between members of that group can signal solidarity. A manager who swears with their team signals informality and access. A manager who swears at their team signals contempt. Same words, categorically different acts.

Beers Fagersten (2012) describes swearing as fundamentally indexical — it points to something beyond its content. It indexes the speaker's identity, the relationship between speaker and listener, and the social context. Same word, completely different meaning depending on who's speaking, to whom, and under what circumstances. This is a theme we'll develop in depth in Module 3.

Section 6

The Tabooness Question: What Happens When Power Fades?

A word's power as profanity depends on its tabooness — the social cost attached to using it. What happens when that cost decreases? How do words lose (or gain) their charge over time?

The euphemism treadmill (introduced in Module 1) gives us one answer: words absorb the stigma of the concepts they describe and eventually need to be replaced. But there is a related process worth naming here: semantic pejoration — when a word that was once neutral or positive acquires negative connotations over time. "Villain" originally simply meant a peasant or serf. "Silly" once meant blessed or fortunate. Words drift toward negativity when they become associated with groups or behaviours that a dominant culture wants to mark as inferior.

And semantic amelioration runs in the other direction: words lose their sting. "Damn" and "hell" were genuinely shocking in polite Victorian company. Now they appear in PG-rated content. The process continues. Understanding these mechanisms is essential for the Bad Word Analysis, where you'll be asked to trace exactly this kind of drift.

Module 03

Profanity, Language & Identity

How do words shape who we are, what we think, and how we think?

💬 Before You Read

Try this: describe yourself using only words other people have used to describe you. How many of those descriptions feel accurate? How many feel like they missed the point, or were meant to diminish you? Language doesn't just describe identity — it participates in creating it. That's what this module is about.

Section 1

Does Language Shape Thought?

The question sounds philosophical, but it has real empirical content. The idea goes by several names: the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis, linguistic relativity, linguistic determinism. The debate about it has been going on for most of the 20th century and remains active.

The strong version — linguistic determinism — claims that language determines thought: you can only think what your language allows you to think. This version has largely been discredited. People clearly have thoughts they struggle to put into words; preverbal infants have mental representations; sign languages and spoken languages support the same cognitive capacities.

The weak version — linguistic relativity — is more durable and more interesting: language influences thought and perception. Having a word for something makes that thing easier to notice, categorize, remember, and communicate. Not having a word for something doesn't make it unthinkable — but it makes it harder to share, to analyze, and to act on.

Lera Boroditsky's research offers compelling evidence. In one well-known study, she found that speakers of languages where "bridge" is a masculine noun (like Spanish) tended to describe bridges with words like "strong," "towering," and "powerful." Speakers of languages where "bridge" is feminine (like German) used words like "elegant," "delicate," and "beautiful" — even when describing identical bridges in a language that has no grammatical gender (Boroditsky, 2018). The grammatical gender of a word in your native language nudges your perception of the thing.

The Irish language has over a dozen words for different types of rain. Arabic has a taxonomically detailed vocabulary for camels — distinguishing not just male and female, but age, number of births, ownership status, whether the animal is about to be slaughtered. People who need to make those distinctions regularly have a linguistic toolkit that supports those distinctions. This is not the same as claiming that English speakers can't perceive drizzle versus downpour — but the vocabulary shapes what gets noticed, communicated, and acted on.

🌍 Global Lens

Wade Davis, the Canadian anthropologist and ethnobotanist, writes compellingly about Indigenous language loss and its relationship to knowledge loss. Languages encode ways of understanding the world — relationships to land, ecological knowledge, systems of meaning — that cannot be fully translated. When a language dies, a worldview dies with it.

The Kenyan author and scholar Ngugi wa Thiong'o extends this argument: colonial education systems didn't just impose a curriculum; they imposed a language that carried the colonizer's categories, values, and hierarchies. To succeed in the colonial system, you had to learn to think in the colonizer's conceptual vocabulary. This is a form of epistemic violence at civilizational scale: entire worldviews rendered unspeakable by the dominance of another language.

Section 2

Language as Performance

One of the most influential ideas in this module comes from the philosopher J.L. Austin, and it asks us to think about language not as description but as action. Austin (1962) introduced the concept of speech acts — the idea that saying something is doing something.

When a judge says "I sentence you to ten years," that sentence doesn't just describe a sentencing. It is the sentencing. When someone says "I promise," the act of saying it is the act of promising. Austin called these performative utterances. Language, in this view, is not a neutral conveyor of meaning — it is an action with effects in the world.

Austin's framework distinguishes three dimensions of every utterance. Click an utterance below to see how these dimensions play out differently depending on context — and how the same words can do completely different things.

Locutionary
What is literally said
The literal content: it is cold in this room.
Illocutionary
What the speaker intends
Could be a request to close the window, turn on heat, or simply an expression of discomfort. The intention is not contained in the words themselves.
Perlocutionary
What the saying does
The window gets closed, or it doesn't. The heater goes on. Or nothing happens. The actual effect — which may or may not match the speaker's intention.

This framework is important for understanding profanity because the same utterance can have completely different illocutionary and perlocutionary dimensions depending on who says it, to whom, in what context, and with what historical weight. A slur directed at someone on the street and the same word analyzed in a linguistics classroom are doing categorically different things — even if the sound waves are identical.

Judith Butler extends Austin's framework into a broader theory of performativity — the idea that identity itself is performed through language and repeated social action rather than being an essence we possess. In Gender Trouble (1990), Butler argues that gender is not something you are but something you do — a set of repeated behaviours and performances that, through repetition, come to feel natural and inevitable. The "doing" precedes the "being."

Section 3

Words as Weapons: Butler and Excitable Speech

In Excitable Speech: A Politics of the Performative (1997), Butler turns to the question of injurious language — slurs, hate speech, and words used to harm. Her core claim: words don't just describe social subjects, they construct them. A slur does not simply reflect a negative view of a person or group. It actively positions that person or group within a social hierarchy. It "hails" them into a subjugated identity.

When someone is called a slur, something happens beyond the communication of contempt. The person is assigned a position in a social order — rendered less-than, categorized as belonging to a stigmatized group, addressed in a way that calls on a history of violence and exclusion. Butler draws on the philosopher Louis Althusser's concept of interpellation: the idea that ideology works by calling out to individuals and assigning them to social positions. The slur interpellates. It says: "You are this thing we have decided is lesser."

Section 4

Epistemic Injustice: When Language Silences

Philosopher Miranda Fricker (2007) gives us additional tools through her concept of epistemic injustice — injustice that operates through knowledge and language, specifically through the practice of not believing certain people or not providing them with the concepts they need to make sense of their own experience.

Type 01

Testimonial Injustice

Someone's testimony is given less credibility because of their identity — not because of anything specific to the quality of their account, but because of who they are and who they're speaking to or about.

Anita Hill's testimony about Clarence Thomas's conduct in 1991 was dismissed as unreliable and overly emotional — not because of anything specific to her testimony, but because of the intersection of her race and gender identity in relation to his.
Type 02

Hermeneutical Injustice

Someone lacks the conceptual vocabulary to describe their own experience, because that vocabulary hasn't been developed — often because the experience is not shared by those with the power to name things and build shared frameworks.

The concept of "sexual harassment" did not enter the mainstream vocabulary until the 1970s. Before that, workers — mostly women — experiencing what we would now call harassment had no widely recognized name for it, no framework for understanding it as a systemic wrong rather than an individual misfortune.

The connection to profanity and taboo language is direct. When certain communities lack the conceptual vocabulary to name what has been done to them through language — when calling something a "slur" or "hate speech" is dismissed as oversensitivity — that is hermeneutical injustice operating in real time. The framework for understanding the harm doesn't exist or isn't recognized, so the harm goes unnamed, and therefore unremedied.

To succeed in the colonial system, you had to learn to think in the colonizer's conceptual vocabulary. Module 04

Profanity, Power & Control

How is language controlled? How has it been used to control people?

💬 Before You Read

Make a list of five things you know you're "not supposed to say" — at school, at work, online, with family. Now for each one: who made that rule? What was being protected? Was it a person, a value, an institution, or a power structure? The answers tell you a lot about how language control actually works.

Section 1

Language Control Is Thought Control

This is the module that gets under the skin a little, because it asks you to look at "appropriate language" rules not as neutral good manners but as exercises of power.

The core claim, which George Orwell dramatized in 1984 (1949) and scholars have been developing ever since: if you control the words available to express an idea, you constrain the ability to think and communicate that idea clearly. Language control is not just about what people say. It shapes what people can articulate, organize around, and resist. Orwell's fictional "Newspeak" was designed with exactly this purpose — a language engineered to make dissent literally unthinkable by eliminating the words through which dissent could be expressed.

This does not mean all language regulation is sinister. There are often times where language regulation is used to protect — hate speech laws, for instance, exist to prevent real harm to real people. The question is always worth asking: what is being protected, from what, and for whose benefit? The honest answer is sometimes "vulnerable people." Sometimes it is "existing power structures." Usually it is both simultaneously, and the task is to sort out which is which.

Section 2

Three Categories of Language Control

Language control operates at three overlapping levels. Click each category to expand.

⚖️ Legal & Political ▾

Governments regulate language through laws, and the limits of that regulation are genuinely contested. In Canada, the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms (Section 2b) guarantees freedom of expression — but the Supreme Court has upheld restrictions that are "reasonable and justifiable in a free and democratic society." Those restrictions include:

  • Hate speech directed at identifiable groups
  • Defamatory libel
  • Obscenity
  • Incitement to violence

The line between legitimate restriction and politically motivated censorship is not always obvious. Laws against blasphemy have been used against heretics, reformers, and scientists. Obscenity laws have been applied to literature, political speech, and art.

The concept of compelled speech adds another dimension: not just what you can't say, but what you must say. Debates about pronoun usage are often framed as compelled speech arguments — but colonized peoples required to use colonial titles and languages were facing compelled speech of a very different magnitude.

👥 Social & Cultural ▾

Social norms, shame, and the threat of exclusion do more daily work in regulating language than any law. You don't say certain words not because they're illegal but because you know what will happen to your relationships, your reputation, and your sense of belonging if you do.

Educational institutions teach children the grammar of both language and propriety simultaneously. Grades, discipline, and "respect" training are all forms of language socialization.

One particularly important mechanism: respectability politics — the strategy by which marginalized groups are encouraged (or pressure themselves) to conform to dominant standards of language and behaviour in order to be taken seriously. The implicit bargain: speak "properly," and we will grant you some recognition. The cost: accept the framing that your own language and cultural expression are inferior.

The WNBA controversy involving Angel Reese and Caitlin Clark illustrates this. Reese, a Black woman, was criticized for trash talk simultaneously described as unsportsmanlike and unladylike. Clark — a white woman — was praised for "competitive fire." The behaviour was similar. The framing was not.

💻 Technological & Corporate ▾

The newest and in many ways most interesting category, because the mechanisms are less visible and more pervasive than either legal prohibition or social shaming.

Content moderation — the automated and human review of online speech — operates at unprecedented scale. Algorithms flag, deprioritize, or remove content that violates platform policies. Shadow-banning (making a user's content invisible to others without informing them) creates a chilling effect: users who aren't sure whether they're being suppressed self-censor preemptively (Bedi, 2021).

TikTok's content moderation systems have reportedly flagged words like "gay," "lesbian," "trans," and "queer" — not slurs, but the neutral self-describing language of LGBTQ+ users. Creators have responded with code-switching and minced oaths: "unalive" for "suicide," "le dollar bean" for a specific slur, "camping" for certain LGBTQ+ contexts. The censorship didn't stop the conversation; it made it harder, more exhausting, and less visible.

🤔 Think About It

The blues, rock and roll, hip-hop — each of these musical forms was censored, suppressed, or dismissed as dangerous at various points in American cultural history. Each of them, not coincidentally, was primarily created by Black artists. The censorship was rarely just about the words. Who benefits from the censorship of these forms? What power structure does the censorship protect? What was the cost to the artists and communities involved?

Section 3

Slurs as a Special Category

Slurs deserve their own treatment because they operate differently from other forms of taboo language. A slur is a word or expression used to insult, dehumanize, or marginalize people based on their identity — race, gender, sexuality, religion, ability, class, nationality. Several features distinguish slurs from other offensive language.

Feature 01
They target groups ▾

"You're an asshole" is a particularist insult — directed at one person's behaviour in a specific moment. A racial slur is a general pejorative — it targets not the individual's behaviour but their membership in a group. The whole group is being attacked through the individual.

Feature 02
They carry history ▾

Slurs are not just rude words. They are the distillate of centuries of discrimination, violence, and dehumanization. This is why their harm cannot be explained purely by the intentions of any single speaker. A speaker who "doesn't mean anything by it" is still deploying the accumulated weight of that history.

Feature 03
They reinforce hierarchies ▾

Slurs position their targets within a hierarchy. They do not just describe that hierarchy; they enact and reinforce it. Every use of a slur is a small act of social positioning — asserting who belongs at the bottom of a social order.

Feature 04
They harm without intent ▾

Even a slur said carelessly, "as a joke," or "not meaning anything by it" can cause real harm to people for whom the word carries real history. The harm is not located in the speaker's intention but in the word's relationship to the group it targets and the history that relationship carries.

A related phenomenon: coded slurs (sometimes called dog whistles). These are terms that function as slurs but are deniable on the surface. "Urban," "thug," "globalist elite," "groomer" — each of these has been used in ways that target specific groups while the speaker can claim they meant nothing of the sort. The harm is embedded; the plausible deniability is the point.

Section 4

Linguistic Imperialism and the Violence of Language Loss

Robert Phillipson's concept of linguistic imperialism — the transfer of a dominant language to other peoples as a tool of colonization and control — gives us a framework for understanding the most extreme form of language control: the forced replacement of one language with another (Phillipson, 1992).

The process follows a consistent pattern: military and political occupation establishes control over territory. The dominant language is imposed through institutions — schools, courts, churches, governments. Indigenous languages are stigmatized, penalized, or banned. Over generations, language loss occurs, carrying with it everything that language encoded: ecological knowledge, relational systems, spiritual frameworks, humour, memory.

In Canada, the residential school system explicitly deployed language as a tool of "civilizing" — removing children from their communities, forbidding them to speak their languages, and punishing them for doing so. The Truth and Reconciliation Commission of Canada's report documents this history in detail. The ongoing effects — on individuals, communities, and the languages themselves, many of which are now critically endangered — are a direct consequence of this policy (Truth and Reconciliation Commission of Canada, 2015).

The broader lesson for this course: the classification of certain language as "inferior," "savage," "obscene," or "inappropriate" has served and continues to serve political purposes. Who decides that a language is civilized is always someone with power. Who pays the price when a language is suppressed is always the people who spoke it.

Module 05

Profanity, Power & Liberation

How have communities reclaimed profanity — and what are the limits?

💬 Before You Read

Think about the word "queer." If you're old enough, you might remember when it was exclusively an insult. If you're younger, you may have grown up with it as a neutral or positive identifier. If you belong to the community it describes, your relationship to the word may be complicated in ways that are specific to your generation, your family, your region. That complexity — who gets to use a word, what it means when they do, and whether the meaning has actually changed — is the focus of this final module.

Section 1

Two Ways to Change Discourse

When a word is being used to harm you, you have two broad options. You can try to change the word, or you can try to change what the word does.

Adrienne Rich, the poet and essayist, wrote: "This is the oppressor's language, yet I need it to talk to you." The problem with abandoning a language entirely — even a colonial one, even one drenched in historical harm — is that you need language to organize, to communicate, to resist. The tools are already in your hands. The question is whether you can repurpose them.

Path A — Change the Word

Euphemism / Replacement

Find a new, neutral term. Leave the old one behind. This is the impulse behind the euphemism treadmill.

Old term becomes too stigmatized to use
New neutral term introduced
New term absorbs stigma over time
Cycle repeats

The problem: the stigma travels with the concept, not the word. Simply renaming doesn't change the underlying social reality.

Path B — Change the Meaning

Reclamation / Resignification

Keep the word. Change what it does — who uses it, what it signals, what associations it carries.

Target community uses the word differently
Repeated use in new context shifts meaning
Word's screen is rescreened (see Section 3)
Word acquires new valence

The problem: the old meaning doesn't disappear. The word remains dangerous in other contexts and mouths.

Section 2

Reclamation and Resignification

Linguistic reclamation is the process by which a targeted group semantically takes back a pejorative term — changing its meaning, neutralizing its sting, or transforming it into a badge of pride. The critical detail: the intervention comes from the group targeted by the word. It is not available to outsiders in the same way.

Resignification is slightly different: the assignment of new meaning to a word or symbol by anyone, not just the target group. When a community reclaims a slur, they are doing something with particular political and historical stakes — repossessing a weapon that was used against them. When an outside group resignifies a word that was never aimed at them, the dynamics are fundamentally different.

The history of "queer" is the most-discussed example. For much of the 20th century, "queer" was used as a derogatory term for gay and lesbian people — sometimes violently. Beginning in the late 1980s and accelerating through the 1990s, activists (particularly through groups like Queer Nation) began deploying the word defiantly and proudly: "We're here, we're queer, get used to it." The word was turned back against the people who had used it as a weapon. Today, "queer" functions as a neutral or positive identifier for many people in LGBTQ+ communities, and as an academic umbrella term for non-normative gender and sexuality. The reclamation is not complete — some people within those communities still find the word painful — but it is demonstrably real.

Philosopher Judith Butler's concept of linguistic exorcism is relevant here: the idea that naming what has been used against you can reduce its power to harm. "One casts out devils by naming them." By claiming the word, speaking it aloud, and recontextualizing it within a community of solidarity rather than a context of attack, communities can begin to metabolize the historical trauma it carries. Not eliminate it — transform the relationship to it.

🌍 Global Lens

Michel Foucault wrote about what he called "reverse discourse" — the process by which a group that has been defined and controlled by dominant discourse begins to speak back using the same terms, but from a different position. He observed that the very categorizations used by 19th-century psychiatry and jurisprudence to pathologize homosexuality gave gay people a language through which to organize and speak in their own behalf.

The Yoruba concept of egan — language as deliberate destabilization of hierarchy — offers a parallel framework from an entirely different cultural tradition: transgressive speech as structured political act, not mere offense. The parallel suggests that using language to resist domination is not a Western innovation; it is a near-universal human capacity.

Section 3

Terministic Screens: Kenneth Burke's Framework

The American rhetorician Kenneth Burke (1966) gave us one of the most useful tools for understanding how reclamation works: terministic screens. Burke's idea: every word we use to describe something simultaneously performs three functions. It selects certain aspects of reality to highlight, it reflects those aspects back to the observer, and it deflects attention away from others.

A word is not a neutral label. It is a lens — a screen through which reality is filtered. And crucially, different screens applied to the same thing produce different realities. 🚫 Deflects

Burke's framework allows us to understand reclamation as terministic rescreening: not changing the word, but changing the screen through which it operates. The word "queer" used to select for deviance, abnormality, and shame. Rescreened, it selects for community, resistance to normalization, and pride. The letters haven't changed. The screen has. What gets reflected — and what gets deflected — is entirely different.

This is also why slurs can be simultaneously harmful and, in the right contexts and mouths, expressions of solidarity. The word hasn't changed; the screen through which it is being deployed has. Context doesn't just modify the meaning — it determines which terministic screen is in operation.

Section 4

Goals and Mechanisms of Reclamation

Not all reclamation is doing the same thing. Researchers have identified at least three distinct goals, each with a different relationship to the word's original derogatory meaning. Click each to explore.

Value Reversal ▾

The pejorative meaning is inverted. "Bad" becomes "good." "Queer" moves from insult to source of pride. "Nasty" is reclaimed by feminist discourse as a term of power. The word retains its emotional force but the valence — the positive or negative charge — flips completely.

"We're here, we're queer, get used to it." The word's transgressive energy is redirected into defiance and pride rather than shame.
Neutralization ▾

The word loses its derogatory sting without necessarily becoming positive — it just stops being a slur. This is rarer and harder to achieve. It requires widespread social change in how the word is heard and processed. It can happen through habituation, through institutional change, or through generational shift.

"Bitch" in many contexts has undergone partial neutralization, particularly in music and casual speech. It remains context-dependent and contested, but its derogatory charge is significantly reduced in some communities.
Stigma Exploitation ▾

The word retains its derogatory edge and is used ironically, defiantly, within the in-group. The derogation is acknowledged and weaponized rather than erased. The implicit message: "We're not what you think that word means — but even if we were, so what." The stigma becomes the armor.

In-group use of the n-word in certain contexts functions as stigma exploitation: the word's history of violence is acknowledged rather than erased, and wielded on the group's own terms rather than the oppressor's.

Redemptive vs. Restrictive Reclamation

Not all words are equally reclaimable, and not all reclamation works the same way. A useful distinction:

Type A

Redemptive Reclamation

Examples: "queer," "Black" as a positive identity term. The word is rescreened to draw out positive capabilities. It is broadly accepted within the community, used by people outside the community in appropriate contexts, and carries genuine pride.

The derogation is accepted and celebrated as part of what makes the community distinct from the norms it refuses. Outside use is generally acceptable in contexts of solidarity and analysis.
Type B

Restrictive Reclamation

Examples: the n-word, "f*g." The word's derogatory nature is retained and used ironically and defiantly, but only within the in-group. Outsiders using it are not participating in the reclamation — they are causing the original harm.

Ta-Nehisi Coates explains this: context determines everything, but context is something you earn through relationship and shared experience. The word does not mean the same thing coming from different mouths — even if the sounds are identical.

The Kendrick Lamar concert incident is a useful case study: at a concert, Lamar invited a white fan onstage, and the fan sang the n-word directly from Lamar's lyrics. Lamar stopped the fan and explained why that was not something that could happen in that space. The fan was not malicious. The explanation was gracious. But the moment illustrated precisely the asymmetry that restrictive reclamation depends on — and why it is not arbitrariness but logic.

Section 5

Profanity as Political Resistance

Beyond reclamation of specific slurs, profanity and transgressive language have a broader history as tools of political resistance. The logic is direct: if you are being policed, controlled, or silenced, speaking in ways that violate the rules of your oppressor is itself a political act. The transgression is the message.

N.W.A.'s "Fuck tha Police" (1988) is a foundational case study. The song — a first-person narrative of police harassment directed at young Black men in Compton — was more than controversial content. The title itself was the point. Using the word "fuck" in direct address to the institution of policing was a deliberate violation of respectability politics: a refusal to petition politely, to speak "properly," to ask nicely for recognition. The language performed the argument. You cannot simultaneously demand to be heard by power and speak in its preferred register.

The FBI sent warning letters to Priority Records following the song's release, arguing it encouraged violence against law enforcement. Annalise Green, analyzing the song's rhetoric, argues that the FBI's response misread the text: it interpreted the song as a threat rather than recognizing it as a specific Black rhetorical tradition of speaking truth to power — a tradition that runs from the blues through jazz through hip-hop (Green, n.d.). The FBI's reading was not neutral. It was a reading through the terministic screen of authority, in which any language that refused deference became "dangerous."

The song connects directly to Burke's terministic screens, Butler's performativity, and Fine and Corte's analysis of swearing as group solidarity. "Fuck tha Police" was not random vulgarity. It was a rhetorical act with a specific illocutionary force: we see what you are doing, we know what you are doing, and we will not perform submission to it. The profanity was the instrument of that performance.

🤔 Think About It

Audre Lorde wrote: "The master's tools will never dismantle the master's house." She was arguing that you cannot use the frameworks and language of an oppressive system to fundamentally challenge that system — at best, you can make cosmetic changes.

Reclamation uses the oppressor's language. Is Lorde right that this limits what reclamation can achieve? Or is Burke's terministic rescreening a counter-argument: that the screen can change even if the word stays the same? Can the master's tools be rescreened — or do they always carry the master's logic?

Section 6

The Limits of Reclamation

Reclamation is real, it is documented, and it matters. But it is not magic, and it has genuine limits worth taking seriously.

Limit 01
Internal disagreement ▾

Not everyone in the targeted group agrees. Generational, regional, and individual differences within communities mean that reclamation is always contested. Some people find reclaimed slurs empowering. Others find them retraumatizing regardless of who says them. There is no consensus, and there may never be.

Limit 02
Outsiders retain access ▾

A word can be reclaimed within a community while remaining a weapon in the hands of people outside it. The reclamation changes the word's meaning in some contexts but not all. The original harm does not disappear.

Limit 03
Commercial appropriation ▾

When a reclaimed term enters mass-market pop culture and is adopted by advertisers, television writers, and brands, who benefits? The community that risked real harm to reclaim the word, or the commercial machine that has repackaged their resistance for profit? The history of hip-hop, queer culture, and Black vernacular English is full of this dynamic.

Limit 04
Change is slow and uneven ▾

Even with enormous cultural effort, meanings change slowly. A word reclaimed in urban centres may retain its full derogatory charge in other contexts. There is no moment when reclamation is "complete." The work is ongoing, contested, and contextually variable.

None of this means reclamation fails. It means reclamation is a struggle, not a solution — and a struggle whose stakes are different for different people within the affected community. The frameworks from this module (terministic screens, performativity, speech acts, epistemic injustice) give you the tools to analyze that struggle with precision.

The Course in Five Positions

What we faced — and what that means for what we can change.

Five modules. Five questions. Five positions on what profanity is, what it does, and what it reveals about the society that produces it. Here is where we've been.

Module 1
Origins

Profanity comes from taboo — the forbidden, the sacred, the transgressive. Its power is not inherent in any word. It is borrowed from the community that makes the word powerful, from the social boundary the word crosses, and from the history that boundary encodes. "Profane" means outside the temple. The temple changes. The structure of transgression remains.

Module 2
Purpose

Profanity serves real purposes — physiological, psychological, social. It is not meaningless noise. It regulates pain and emotion, marks group belonging, signals trust, and structures social hierarchies. Understanding what swearing does — intrapersonally and interpersonally — changes how we hear it and complicates simple arguments for its suppression.

Module 3
Identity

Language and identity are entangled at the root. Words do not just describe social positions; they create and reinforce them through performance, repetition, and the accumulated weight of history. Epistemic injustice — when language denies people the concepts to name their own experience — is one of the most pervasive and least visible forms of harm.

Module 4
Control

Language regulation is always political. Who controls language controls what can be thought, articulated, and resisted. The history of linguistic imperialism in Canada is not ancient history — it is present in the enduring effects of residential schools, in critically endangered Indigenous languages, and in the ongoing question of whose speech gets policed and whose goes unremarked.

Module 5
Liberation

Language can be reclaimed, rescreened, repurposed. The transgression that made profanity dangerous can be redirected as resistance. Reclamation is not magic — it is contested, partial, and slow. But it is real. And it is the place where all five modules converge: the question is not just what words do to us, but what we can do with words.

"Not everything that is faced can be changed, but nothing can be changed until it is faced." — James Baldwin

Language is part of what we have to face. The words we use — the ones we forbid, the ones we deploy, the ones we flinch at and the ones we've learned to wield — are not decorations on top of social reality. They are part of its structure. That is what makes them worth studying, and worth questioning. That is what CULT 2300 has been about.
